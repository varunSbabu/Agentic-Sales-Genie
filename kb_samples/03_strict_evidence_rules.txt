STRICT EVIDENCE QUOTING RULES v1.0
====================================

These rules govern how the agent records EVIDENCE in dimension_scores,
strengths, and improvements. Violations break the analysis.


RULE 1: VERBATIM ONLY
---------------------
Every quoted phrase in `evidence`, `strengths`, or `improvements` must
appear character-exact in the call transcript.

Acceptable:
  Rep said "Did you receive a mailer from us?" → evidence is exactly that.

NOT acceptable:
  Rep asked about the mailer.   (paraphrased, not quoted)
  Rep said "Did you get our mailer?"   (rewritten, not exact)


RULE 2: NO BORROWING FROM FRAMEWORKS
------------------------------------
The other documents in this knowledge base contain hypothetical example
phrases — they describe DIFFERENT, NON-REAL calls. The following phrases
are FRAMEWORK ILLUSTRATIONS and must NEVER appear in your analysis unless
they are also literally present in the transcript being evaluated:

  - "manual CSV pulls"
  - "automation pitch"
  - "we lose maybe 20% of new users"
  - "in revenue terms"
  - "twenty percent"
  - "decision criteria"
  - "champion-only relationship"
  - "MEDDIC"
  - any phrase wrapped in <example>...</example> tags

If you see yourself about to write one of these in a strength or evidence
field, STOP. Re-read the actual transcript. Use what THIS rep said.


RULE 3: SPEAKER LABEL FORMAT
----------------------------
When quoting, include the speaker label exactly as in the transcript:
  "Rep: Thank you for calling Nissan."
  "Prospect: I want to update my map."

Do NOT invent a speaker label. Do NOT drop the colon. Do NOT add
ellipses unless they were in the source.


RULE 4: BRIEF QUOTES, NOT ENTIRE TURNS
--------------------------------------
Evidence quotes should be SHORT (under 25 words) and surgical. Quote the
exact sentence that supports your point, not the whole speaking turn.

  Whole turn (too long): "Rep: Alright, well, here are a few reasons to
  consider purchasing today. It looks as though you haven't updated your
  vehicle for 3 years, so that would be the equivalent of getting 3 years'
  worth of updates for the price of one. In addition, special offers like
  the current promotion don't come around too often."

  Surgical (good): "Rep: It looks as though you haven't updated your
  vehicle for 3 years, so that would be the equivalent of getting 3 years'
  worth of updates for the price of one."


RULE 5: STRENGTHS AND IMPROVEMENTS ARE ABOUT THIS REP
-----------------------------------------------------
Strengths and improvements must describe behavior that THIS rep did or
failed to do in THIS specific transcript. Each one should cite a moment
from the transcript.

  Bad strength (generic): "Rep showed good rapport."
  Bad strength (borrowed): "Rep paraphrased the prospect's quote about
    CSV pulls before responding."
  Good strength: "Rep acknowledged John's hesitation about cost and then
    explicitly offered a $50 discount to reduce friction, as in 'I would
    definitely recommend taking advantage of the extra $50 off before it
    expires.'"

  Bad improvement (generic): "Could ask better questions."
  Bad improvement (borrowed): "Should have followed up with quantification
    in revenue terms."
  Good improvement: "When the prospect said 'I'm not really sure if I
    can afford it right now', the rep moved straight to discount tactics
    instead of asking what was making it feel unaffordable."


RULE 6: SCORE THE TRANSCRIPT, NOT YOUR GUESS OF THE CALL
--------------------------------------------------------
You only see what's in the transcript. If the transcript ends mid-sentence
or lacks a section (e.g. no greeting was captured), score what's present
without assuming what happened in the missing parts.

If the transcript is short or partial, prefer LOWER scores for dimensions
where you'd need more evidence to score high. Better to be conservative
than to project quality that isn't visible.
